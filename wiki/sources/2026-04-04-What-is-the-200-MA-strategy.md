What is the 200 MA Strategy? It represents the average closing price over the past 200 trading days and helps reveal the long-term trend by filtering out short-term market fluctuations. When price trades above the average, the market is typically viewed as being in an uptrend; when it trades below, the trend is considered negative.
The concept is very simple:
• Buy when the price closes above the 200-day moving average
• Sell when the price closes below it
In essence, this is a classic trend-following approach. Its main purpose is risk control - helping investors avoid major bear markets rather than outperform a buy-and-hold strategy.

The strategy delivers an annual return of 6.7%, slightly below the buy-and-hold return of 7.4% (dividends excluded). You are invested about 70% of the time, which gives a risk-adjusted return of 9.5% (CAGR divided by the time spent in the market). Maximum drawdown is 29%, compared with 56% for buy-and-hold, meaning the strategy offers better performance on a risk-adjusted basis.
Why it works
The 200-day moving average helps traders stay invested during extended bull markets while avoiding large losses during major declines. Historical backtests on the S&P 500 show that this approach achieves long-term returns similar to buy-and-hold but with drawdowns roughly half as severe.
The downside
In sideways markets, the strategy can generate frequent false signals, leading to “whipsaws” and extra trades. During strong bull markets without interruptions, it may underperform because it exits too early and re-enters too late. A clear example of this is the gold price (GLD):
There are many false signals along the way, but eventually you capture the big moves of a strong bull market. Like with the S&P 500, the basic 200-day moving average strategy underperforms buy-and-hold in raw returns: 7.2% vs. 11.5%. However, it performs better on a risk-adjusted basis, reaching 11.7%.
How traders improve it
Many traders use the 200-day moving average as a trend filter rather than a standalone system:
• Only take long trades when price is above the 200 DMA
• Combine it with other indicators, such as RSI or shorter moving averages
• Use moving average crossovers, like the 50-day crossing the 200-day average
Bull vs. bear markets
Market behavior differs greatly between bull and bear phases. In bull markets, volatility usually declines, while in bear markets it tends to rise. S&P 500 data from 1993 onward illustrates this:
Above the 200-day moving average: average daily range 1.05%
Below the 200-day moving average: average daily range 2.1%
This shows that price action is very different depending on whether the market trades above or below its long-term average.
A bear and bull market 200 MA strategy
For our paid subscribers (#90), we offer a strategy that takes advantage of higher volatility in bear markets:

The strategy has performed well, even in several out-of-sample years.
Key takeaway
The 200-day moving average isn’t a magic formula. Its true value lies in risk management, helping you avoid major bear markets while still participating in long-term uptrends.
