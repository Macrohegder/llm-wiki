Bollinger Bands are a versatile technical analysis tool designed to measure market volatility and identify potential price movements. Developed by John Bollinger in the 1980s, they help traders determine if an asset is potentially overbought or oversold relative to its recent price history.
At the bottom, you find a backtested trading strategy based on Bollinger Bands.
How They Work
The indicator consists of three primary components:
The Middle Band:
A simple moving average (SMA), which usually defaults to a 20-day period.
The Upper and Lower Bands:
These are plotted two standard deviations away from the middle band.
Because standard deviation is a mathematical measure of volatility, these bands are dynamic; they automatically expand when the market becomes volatile and contract during quieter periods.
Reading Market Volatility: “The Squeeze”
One of the most popular ways to use this tool is by identifying a Bollinger Band Squeeze. This occurs when the upper and lower bands move close together, signifying a period of low volatility. Traders often view this consolidation as a precursor to a significant price breakout in either direction. When the bands begin to widen, it typically indicates the start of a pronounced new trend.
Spotting Overbought and Oversold Conditions
Bollinger Bands act as dynamic support and resistance levels.
Overbought:
When prices reach or breach the upper band, it suggests the market may be overextended, potentially signaling a reversal to the downside.
Oversold:
Conversely, when prices touch or fall below the lower band, the asset is often considered oversold, which may present a buying opportunity or a “bounce” back toward the mean.
Key Trading Strategies
Traders utilize several strategies based on these bands, including:
Bollinger Band Breakouts:
Entering a trade when the price moves outside the bands, suggesting a shift in market momentum.
Bollinger Band Walks:
Identifying strong trends where the price “walks” along the upper or lower band for an extended period.
Double Bollinger Bands:
Using two sets of bands (with 1 and 2 standard deviations) to create specific “buy” and “sell” zones.
Important Limitations
For mean reversion strategies, it’s common to buy when the price hits the lower band and sell when it reaches the upper band. However, this strategy often leads to huge losses because in downtrends the price never hits the upper band. Thus, you might need another sell rule.
Furthermore, Bollinger Bands should not be used as a standalone tool. They are based on past price data and can produce delayed signals or “head fakes”—situations where the price appears to break out but quickly reverses.
