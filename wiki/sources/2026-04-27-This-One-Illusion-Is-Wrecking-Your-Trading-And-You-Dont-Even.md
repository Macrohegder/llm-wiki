I saw one of the coolest optical illusions the other day.
A photo that looks like it’s in full color… but
it’s not
.
The image (below) is
black and white
.
The only color is in the transparent stripes laid over it.
And somehow your brain fills in the rest.
It looks totally real, your brain swears the shirt is blue, the face has warm skin tones, the chairs are green.
But none of that is real.
And that’s what happens in trading..
Our Brain Doesn’t “See”, It Completes
The illusion works because your brain is not a camera.
It’s a prediction machine.
It doesn’t just take in data. It interprets it. Fills in gaps. Adds context. Draws conclusions fast.
In trading, we do the same thing. Constantly.
We see a strong-looking equity curve. We assume the logic is solid.
We see recent winners in backtests. We believe the edge is real.
We see a certain shape on a chart. We feel like “we know” what’s going to happen next.
But just like that photo, the pattern your brain sees might not actually be there.
You're reacting to the
color overlay
, not the real structure underneath.
Cognitive Bias = Mental Illusion
This is the big insight:
Cognitive biases are optical illusions for the mind.
The same way colored lines trick your visual system, mental shortcuts trick your decision-making.
And if you're building trading systems without awareness of this, you’re flying blind with a broken compass.
Let’s Talk Practical Lessons
Here’s how this applies to real-life systematic trading:
1.
Chart Patterns Are Illusions
A beautiful double bottom. A nice bullish engulfing candle. (It’s easy to catch and feels safe because everyone knows about it)
All of that might just be noise, patterns your brain wants to see.
Lesson:
Use visuals to get ideas, not conclusions.
A “pattern” is a suspicion, not proof.
Proof comes from cold, hard stats over many trades and across multiple regimes.

2.
Intuition is a Filter, Not a Fact
Your brain wants certainty. It will fill in gaps with what
feels
right, especially when looking at backtest charts or metrics.
Lesson:
Don’t trust visual strength until it’s statistically verified.
A clean chart means nothing without robustness testing, walk-forward validation, and out-of-sample confirmation.
3.
Biases Look Like Logic Until You Name Them
Confirmation bias. Recency bias. Survivorship bias.
These aren't abstract theory, they're mental overlays.
They color your interpretation of data, just like the illusion colors that grayscale photo.
Lesson:
The moment you become aware of a bias, it loses half its power.
Start journaling your assumptions, your snap judgments, your overconfidence moments.
See your own thoughts from the outside.
4.
You Can Use Illusions, Against the Crowd
Here's the edge part.
Mass psychology
is
a cognitive illusion machine.
Traders will see meaning where there is none.
They’ll see “strength” after a parabolic move.
They’ll see “support” just before a breakdown.
Lesson:
You don’t need to see truth. You just need to know what
others
are falsely seeing, and position accordingly.
Systematic edges often emerge from exploiting crowd illusions.

The goal isn’t to eliminate bias. That’s impossible.
The goal is to
see

How Do You Know Your Own Brain Is Lying?
This is the hardest part.
Because your brain feels right even when it’s wrong. It doesn’t say “this is a guess.” It says “this is true.”
So here’s the trick:
Don’t ask “Does this feel real?”
Ask:
“What would disprove this?”
Test from the outside. Behavior over belief.
Use friction. Use journaling. Use system logic.
Build the habit of treating your thoughts as assumptions, not facts.
Why This Makes You a Sharper Trader
You don’t need a genius IQ.
Train yourself to:
See through illusions (even when they feel “right”)
Trust data over your eyes
Catch the crowd’s bias, and bet against it
Test, test, test, until intuition becomes irrelevant
Because in the markets, it’s not the trader with the best story who wins.
It’s the one who
sees through
the stories.
And you can train that.
