What’s the risk of losing money in the stock market?
Many people think investing in stocks is similar to gambling. However, more than a century of data from the S&P 500 suggests something different. Market risk and large drawdowns are mostly short-term events.
Over longer horizons, those fluctuations tend to fade. Investors who can ignore the day-to-day noise of temporary declines usually find that the long-term odds are strongly in their favor.
Expect market declines
Volatility is not a malfunction of the stock market—it is the cost of participating in it. Temporary declines happen regularly and are a normal part of investing:
5% pullbacks: These are very common and often occur several times within a single year.
10% corrections: Typically appear about once every one to two years.
20% or larger bear markets: Historically occur roughly every five to six years.
Major crashes: Severe downturns such as the 57% decline during the financial crisis in 2008 can be frightening, but the market has historically recovered and eventually moved on to new highs.

Rolling Returns
A useful way to evaluate long-term performance is by looking at rolling returns. This approach calculates the annualized return for every possible starting point in history rather than relying on a single entry date. It reduces the impact of timing luck and provides a more realistic picture of how the market behaves.
When using rolling returns, the data shows that:
Over 10-year periods, the market has delivered positive returns roughly 94% of the time.
The method also captures both the strongest and weakest decades—such as the 1930s or the 2000s—demonstrating that even difficult periods tend to recover if investors remain patient.

The Risk of Losing Money in the Stock Market
The likelihood of experiencing a loss depends largely on how long you stay invested:
1 day: Around a 46% chance of losing money, which is close to a coin toss.
1 year: Roughly a 25% probability of a negative return.
10 years: About a 6% chance of ending with a loss.
20 years: Historically, there has been no 20-year period in U.S. stock market history that produced a nominal loss.
The key lesson
Short-term risk in the stock market is very real, but long-term risk is much smaller than many investors assume. Time is one of the most powerful advantages an investor has.
By investing broadly through index funds and maintaining a time horizon of a decade or more, investors give economic growth, productivity improvements, and reinvested dividends time to work.
Over long periods, these forces tend to transform temporary market declines into positive long-term outcomes (assuming no extreme disruptions such as confiscation or revolutions).
