What happens with SPY if you buy it at the close and sell at the next open?
Take a look at SPY’s performance over the last 25 years:
Left chart:
Buy at close, sell at next open.
Right chart:
Buy at open, sell at close.
On paper, the overnight approach outperforms the stock itself.
With this 200 day SMA regime filter I would add this into my portfolio.
This system often fails in live markets (after fees…).
But by knowing this effect you can still generate a massive alphas.
There are 4 very important things you can gain from understanding this effect in the US markets. But first, you need to understand what it actually is and why it happens.
But First, Why This Effect Happens
The most compelling explanation comes from the
inventory risk management hypothesis
, detailed in a
Federal Reserve Bank of New York Staff Reports
:
Daytime Selling Pressure:
During U.S. trading hours, selling pressure often creates a net negative order imbalance by market close.
Market Maker Absorption:
Risk-averse market makers step in as natural buyers, absorbing this excess inventory onto their books.
Overnight Risk Premium:
Holding this inventory overnight is risky markets are illiquid while you sleep. Market makers demand a slight expected return to compensate.
European Open Resolution:
When European markets open, liquidity surges, allowing market makers to unwind positions creating the “overnight drift.”
Here are my backtest metrics comparing buying at the close versus buying at the open (SPY).
Market gives a tiny overnight reward for holding positions, which compounds over time.
Why ETFs Tried and Failed
This effect was so noticeable that ETFs like
NSPY
and
NIWM
were created to exploit it. Both were liquidated in 2023 because:
Low investor interest:
NSPY ~$3.7M, NIWM ~$1.4M AUM.
Underperformance:
They didn’t beat simply holding S&P 500.
Market awareness:
Everyone knew about the effect, making it hard to profit once crowded.
Knowing about an effect doesn’t make it automatically tradable.
The Big Problem with Overnight Strategies
Here’s where fresh retail traders stumble:
Slippage kills profits:
Even tiny gaps between expected and executed price erode gains.
Commissions matter:
Daily trades quickly add up.
But if you’re wondering, ‘Why bother?’ that’s precisely why many traders give up before they ever uncover a real edge.
