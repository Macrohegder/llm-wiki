7 best algo trading strategies for beginners
is the first step toward trading based on data rather than gut feeling. Most trading advice found online is impossible to backtest, which is exactly why so many newcomers struggle to find consistency.
By shifting your focus to statistics and clear rules, you can move away from “stories” and toward a system you can actually trust.
1. The 200-Day Moving Average Regime Filter
The 200-day moving average is the first indicator legendary investor Paul Tudor Jones looks at because it acts as a primary “trouble sensor”.
The strategy is remarkably simple: buy when the S&P 500 is above the average and exit when it falls below.
Since 1960, this approach has returned 6.6% annually with a maximum drawdown of only 28%, significantly lower than the 56% drawdown seen in a buy-and-hold strategy. It is also a powerful filter for shorter-term strategies. Please read our website (the article has the same name) for more details about this filter.
By only trading pullbacks when the price is above the 200-day MA, you create a much smoother equity curve with smaller, faster-recovering losses.
2. The Golden Cross
A “Golden Cross” occurs when the 50-day moving average crosses above the 200-day average, signaling a bullish trend. This strategy capitalizes on the market’s long-term tendency to trend upward due to productivity and inflation.
Backtested since 1960, this strategy produced a 79% win rate with an average gain per trade of 15.7%. While the annual return of 6.7% is slightly lower than buy-and-hold, it achieves this while only being in the market 70% of the time, offering a superior risk-adjusted return.

3. TQQQ and BTAL Annual Rebalancing
For those looking for a hands-off approach, this strategy requires just one trade per year. By rebalancing to 33% TQQQ (a 3x leveraged Nasdaq-100 ETF) and 67% BTAL (an anti-beta ETF) every January, you balance aggressive growth with crash protection.
Since 2012, this combination has yielded an 18% annual return, beating the Nasdaq in 8 of the last 13 years. Note that TQQQ is highly leveraged, so fees and volatility must be considered.
4. Gold Momentum (Cross-Asset Verification)
This strategy moves beyond looking at gold in isolation by verifying momentum through Treasury yields.
At the end of each month, you calculate the 12-month total return for both gold (GLD) and 10-year Treasuries (IEF). You only stay long on gold if both are positive; otherwise, you move to cash.
Since 1970, this has returned 11.5% annually with a 31% max drawdown—nearly half the drawdown of holding gold alone.

5. RSI Mean Reversion & The “QS Exit”
Short-term traders often use the Relative Strength Index (RSI). A standard 2-day RSI strategy (buying below 10 and selling above 80) yields roughly 9% annually.
However, using the “QS Exit”, selling when the close is higher than yesterday’s high, provides a much smoother ride.
This variation reduces the maximum loss to 23% and significantly improves the recovery factor.
6. The Choppiness Index Filter
Created by Bill Dreiss, the Choppiness Index identifies when a market is sideways (choppy) or trending.
To avoid being caught in sideways “whipsaws,” this strategy only enters a long position when the 15-day Choppiness Index is below 50 (indicating a range-bound market) and the 2-day RSI falls below 20.
This selective approach yields an 8.5% annual return despite being in the market only 15% of the time.

7. Turnaround Tuesday
This seasonal strategy exploits a specific weekly pattern: when the market closes lower on a Monday than Friday’s low, it often “turns around” on Tuesday.
By buying the Monday close and selling when the price exceeds the previous day’s high (or after four days), a $100,000 investment in 1993 would be worth $800,000 today. It has a max drawdown of just 20% while being invested only 11% of the time.
