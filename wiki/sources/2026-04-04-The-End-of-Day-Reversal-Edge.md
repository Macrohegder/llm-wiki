Today, we present an interesting end-of-day reversal effect. This post is based on research by Baltussen, G., Da, Z., & Soebhag, A. (2024). The article is called
End‑of‑Day Reversal
.
What Is the End-of-Day Reversal?
The idea is simple. Stocks that have performed poorly during the day tend to rebound in the final 30 minutes of trading.
Conversely, stocks that have been strong earlier in the session tend to flatten out or underperform into the close. This pattern appears repeatedly in U.S. equity data and has been documented since the early 1990s.
The setup works like this. During the trading day, you measure each stock’s return from the previous close until 3:30 p.m. Eastern Time. You then rank stocks based on that intraday performance. The worst performers, the biggest losers, are placed in one group, and the strongest performers, the biggest winners, in another. From 3:30 to 4:00 p.m., the final half hour of trading, the historical tendency has been for the losers to outperform the winners.
Trading Rules
The authors analyze common stocks (CRSP share codes 10 & 11, price > $5, above the 10th NYSE size percentile) listed on NYSE, NASDAQ, and AMEX from January 1993 through December 2019.

Why Might This Happen?
One explanation involves retail traders. Individual investors often behave in a contrarian way late in the day. Stocks that are down significantly can appear “cheap” or attractive as dip-buying candidates. This can create buying pressure into the close, especially in stocks that have already sold off.
Another explanation relates to short sellers. Professional traders who are short a stock during the day may reduce risk before the closing bell. Covering a short position means buying back shares, which pushes prices up.
Why It Matters for Systematic Traders
That said, this is not a free lunch.
Transaction costs matter enormously. Trading in the last 30 minutes of the session can involve wider spreads, slippage, and competition from high-frequency participants. What looks attractive on a gross basis can shrink meaningfully after realistic costs. Execution quality becomes central to capturing the edge.
Scalability and Limitations
Scalability is another limitation. Much of the historical profitability has been stronger in smaller and less liquid stocks. That naturally caps the size at which the strategy can be deployed. Large institutional capital would struggle to implement it without moving prices.
There is also the usual caveat that once an anomaly becomes widely known, returns may diminish. Some evidence suggests the effect has weakened in more recent years, although it has not completely disappeared. Markets adapt, and edges rarely remain untouched.
