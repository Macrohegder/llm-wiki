Is short interest a bearish or bullish signal?
First, let’s explain what short interest means:
Short interest, the percentage of a company’s shares that have been borrowed and sold by short sellers, is widely viewed as a measure of market sentiment.
However, many traders misunderstand what it actually signals about future returns.
Let’s look at what the data and research really say.
What Does Short Interest Measure?
Short interest represents the number of shares currently sold short but not yet repurchased, expressed as a percentage of total shares outstanding.
Investors also track the short-interest ratio, often called “days to cover.” This metric divides total short interest by average daily trading volume and estimates how many days it would take short sellers to buy back their positions.
An increase in short interest often reflects growing bearish sentiment. It suggests that many market participants believe the stock is overvalued or likely to fall. But the figure itself does not explain why traders are pessimistic.
High Short Interest and Future Returns (Backtest)
A common belief is that high short interest is a contrarian buy signal. The research, however, largely points in the opposite direction.
A frequently cited study, The Predictiveness of Short Interest by Nasdaq (Brian Bruce), analyzed Russell 3000 stocks over roughly two decades. The findings were clear:
Is high short interest a bearish or bullish signal?
Stocks in the highest short-interest decile underperformed the broader market.
Companies with elevated short interest delivered lower subsequent returns than those with low short interest, and this pattern persisted across multiple time periods.
Short interest strategy
The intuition is straightforward. Heavily shorted stocks are often companies facing weak fundamentals or deteriorating prospects. Professional investors who short these stocks typically do so after identifying substantial risks, and, on average, their negative assessment proves justified.
Is High Short Interest a Good or Bad Strategy?
Additional academic research supports these conclusions.
A study published in the Journal of Financial Economics examined stock performance following periods of high short interest. It found that:
Stocks with high short interest tend to experience negative abnormal returns over time.
Stocks with low short interest tend to generate positive abnormal returns.
Importantly, the effect was economically meaningful, not just statistically significant.
Further evidence comes from a December 2016 CFA Institute study titled Short Interest and Aggregate Stock Returns. The authors found that high short interest predicts a lower equity risk premium and weaker future excess returns. In other words, short interest contains genuine information about future performance.
What About Short Squeezes?
High short interest is often associated with the possibility of a short squeeze, as famously seen in the GameStop episode a few years back.
A short squeeze occurs when a rising price forces short sellers to buy back shares to limit losses, accelerating the rally. While squeezes can be dramatic, they are the exception rather than the rule.
Research and market experience show:
Short squeezes are rare and difficult to predict.
High short interest alone does not reliably signal an imminent squeeze.
Most heavily shorted stocks continue to underperform rather than surge.
Trading purely in anticipation of a squeeze is therefore closer to speculation than to a statistically grounded strategy.
Limitations and Context
Short interest is not a perfect indicator. Reporting is delayed and does not reflect real-time positioning. It should never be used in isolation.
Moreover, not all short positions reflect outright bearish bets. Some are part of hedging strategies or arbitrage trades, which complicates interpretation.
Short Interest Strategy: Key Takeaways
High short interest does not consistently predict rallies or short squeezes.
On average, stocks with higher short interest underperform those with lower short interest.
Thus, we have answered whether high short interest is a bearish or bullish signal.
