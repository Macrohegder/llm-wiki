In the world of quantitative finance, there are two distinct religions.
You have the
Trend Followers
(the managed futures crowd, the CTA wizards) who believe in “cutting losses short and letting winners run.” They buy strength. They sell weakness. They are the hunters of outliers.
Then you have the
Mean Reversionists
(the market makers, the short-term swing traders). They believe that panic is temporary. They buy fear. They sell greed. They provide liquidity when no one else will.
But some traders pick a side and stick to it like a football team. Ask yourself: Are you more biased toward trend-following or mean-reversion?
The single most robust way to smooth your equity curve, survive “lost decades” in the market, and leverage the Geometric Compounding Effect is not to choose one. It is to combine them.
In this deep dive, we are going to explore why these two strategies are the perfect “odd couple,” and how combining them unlocks a mathematical phenomenon known as the Rebalancing Bonus.
The Philosophy of Divergence vs. Convergence
To understand why they work together, you must understand why they work individually.
1. Trend Following (Divergence)
Trend following is
convex
. It has a “long volatility” profile.
Win Rate:
Low (10% - 40%).
Payoff:
Massive. You lose small amounts frequently, but occasionally you catch a Nvidia run or a crude oil crash that makes your year.
Psychology:
Hard. You are wrong most of the time. It’s a thousand small cuts before the one key trade.
Risk:
Whipsaw markets (sideways chop).
2. Mean Reversion (Convergence)
Mean reversion is
concave
. It has a “short volatility” profile.
Win Rate:
High (65% - 75%).
Payoff:
Capped. You take quick profits. You are picking up nickels and dimes consistently.
Psychology:
Gratifying. You are right most of the time.
Risk:
“Left Tail” events. Trying to catch a falling knife that keeps getting heavier. (e.g., buying the dip in 2008, covid crash etc).
Trend Following (Positive Skew) vs. Mean Reversion (Negative Skew). Combining them neutralizes the skew risk.

The Magic of Uncorrelation
The “Holy Grail” of investing, according to
Ray Dalio
, is finding 15 uncorrelated return streams.
Beginners try to diversify by buying Apple and Microsoft. That is not a good diversification. In a crash, correlations go to 1.
However,
Trend and Mean Reversion are structurally negatively correlated.
When markets are trending strongly (2020-2021), Mean Reversion strategies often get run over. They keep shorting new highs or buying dips that keep dipping. When markets are choppy and range-bound (2011-2012), Trend strategies get chopped to pieces. They buy the breakout, it fails. They sell the breakdown, it bounces. But Mean Reversion thrives in that chop. When you overlay these equity curves, the drawdowns of one are usually funded by the profits of the other.

The “Rebalancing Bonus” (Shannon’s Demon)
Here is where the compound effect.
Claude Shannon, the father of information theory, proposed a concept now called “Shannon’s Demon.” He proved that you could take two assets with
zero expected return
, but if they were uncorrelated and volatile, you could generate a
positive return
simply by rebalancing between them.
(Even Anthropic named Claude AI after Claude Shannon, the father of information theory.)
This is called Volatility Harvesting.
https://breakingthemarket.com/the-great-age-of-rebalancing-begins/
If you have a Trend strategy (high vol) and a Mean Reversion strategy (high vol), and you rebalance them 50/50 monthly:
When Trend rips higher and Mean Reversion draws down, you sell some Trend (sell high) and add to Mean Reversion (buy low).
When Trend gets whipped out in a chop and Mean Reversion recovers, you sell the Reversion profits and reload the Trend strategy.
You are mechanically forcing yourself to buy low and sell high between your strategies.
