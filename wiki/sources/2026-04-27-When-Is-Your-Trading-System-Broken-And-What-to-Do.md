Let’s Talk: Is Your Trading System Broken?
Every systematic trader eventually faces this:
Your backtest looks great. You go live. It works.
Then one day… it doesn’t.
The wins slow down.
The drawdowns get deeper.
You start to ask:
“Did the edge disappear? Or is this just normal?”
This is a Nobel Prize–level question, and if you trade long enough, you’ll eventually ask it too.
Here’s how to think about it in plain language.
First: What Type of Strategy Are You Running?
Not all drawdowns mean a strategy is “broken.” The context matters.
Type 1: Alpha-Specific (Fragile) Strategies
These strategies often rely on:
Fast market microstructure inefficiencies.
Information decay.
Niche patterns that others haven’t exploited (yet).
Example: An open-range breakout that fires in the first 1-minute and depends on latency advantage.
If a strategy like this starts failing suddenly and consistently
, it may mean:
Someone else has stepped in, the information has been arbitraged away, or the market structure has shifted in a way that nullifies your speed advantage. In these scenarios, the system isn't just in a drawdown; it's fundamentally broken because its core premise has been invalidated.
This is how edges die quietly.
In this image is Pairs Trading Strategy
Lesson:
If your strategy depends on being the first to act, you’re in a race. And you might’ve just lost it.
Type 2: Long-Term (Robust) Strategies
Now, let's talk about
long-term strategies
, especially those with a long-side bias (like many equity strategies).
Stocks, over the long haul, tend to have an upward bias.
In these strategies,
drawdowns are totally normal
.
Your strategy rarely "totally fails" in the same way a fast-alpha strategy can.
Think about it:
if you randomly picked five solid stocks and held them for a year, you’d probably make money.
Long-term, things tend to go up, but in the short term, you'll absolutely experience drawdowns, and you have to accept them as part of the game.
Our SetupAlpha strategies are also built on clear logic, designed to compound over time.
But how do you differentiate between a healthy, albeit painful, drawdown and a truly broken long-term strategy?
The Power of Portfolio Diversification: Your First Line of Defense
This brings us to a crucial point:
we highly suggest trading strategies in a portfolio
. This is precisely why we rely on tools like RealTest - to build and manage diversified portfolios of strategies.
Consider this:
If you have multiple strategies, say five SetupAlpha strategies, and one of them starts losing money, your overall portfolio is likely still making money. The losses from that single strategy don't affect your overall profitability much. You can ride out that rough patch, understanding that the market regime for that specific strategy might not be ideal right now, but it will likely start working again soon.
However, if you trade
only one strategy or a set of very correlated strategies
, and that one strategy starts losing money, you don't have other strategies to pick up the slack. Now you're in the red, and the pressure is on.
Some traders say: you could set a maximum drawdown limit for your strategies, say 20%, and stop trading if it's hit. But honestly, this rule doesn't always make much sense in isolation, especially for robust long-term strategies that naturally experience deeper drawdowns.
Our Own Approach at SetupAlpha: Adaptive Capital Allocation
One of the biggest mistakes we see in the community is
binary thinking
, either a strategy is “working” or “not working.”
But trading isn't black and white. At SetupAlpha, we don’t
turn strategies off and on
like light switches. We
adjust capital allocation based on live performance
.
Here’s how we do it:
If a strategy starts underperforming relative to the others, we
reduce its allocation
.
If it performs better, we
increase allocation proportionally
.
The worse a strategy performs, the less weight it gets
automatically
.
That way, we don’t have to sit around guessing:
“Is it broken? Should I stop trading it?”
Instead of random judgment calls, we let
performance guide capital flow
.
