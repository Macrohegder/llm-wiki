Why Bond Trading Strategies Deserve More Attention
The bond market is much larger than the stock market, yet many traders overlook it - a missed opportunity.
Trading bonds offers several advantages:
Diversification across asset classes
Low correlation to equities
Smoother portfolio performance
Alternative sources of returns
Bonds often behave differently from stocks, making them excellent portfolio stabilizers rather than standalone profit engines. For systematic traders, this is ideal: strategies that complement each other instead of competing.
What Is a Bond? A Quick Refresher
A bond is essentially a loan that investors make to a government or corporation. In return, the issuer promises:
Regular interest payments (coupon)
Repayment of principal at maturity
However, bonds carry some risks every trader should understand:
Credit risk: the issuer may default
Interest rate risk: rising rates can push bond prices down
Inflation risk: the purchasing power of returns can erode
Understanding interest rate sensitivity is key to trading bonds effectively.
Why Trade Bonds Instead of Just Holding Them?
Many investors see bonds as passive income vehicles, but bond prices actually fluctuate daily due to:
Changes in interest rate expectations
Macroeconomic data releases
Central bank policy decisions
Shifts in risk sentiment
This daily volatility creates opportunities for traders, especially those using systematic strategies.

1. Seasonal Bond Trading Strategy
Seasonality exists even in Treasury bonds. This strategy takes advantage of recurring calendar effects, where bonds historically perform better during certain times of the year.
Trading rules:
Buy at the close of the fourth-to-last trading day of the month
Sell at the close of the last trading day of the month

2. Short Bond Trading Strategy
Unlike most bond strategies that remain long, this approach trades from the short side, taking advantage of periods when bond prices historically decline.
Trading rules:
Sell short at the close of the month
Cover at the close of the seventh trading day of the following month
Important note: TLT pays a dividend on the last day of the trading month. Be sure to adjust for this in backtests, otherwise returns may be overstated.
