The IBS indicator is a powerful indicator you have never heard of. It’s one of the most effective tools for over four decades and has a remarkably simple calculation. Despite its simplicity, it has provided a significant “tail wind” for mean-reverting strategies in the stock market.
What is the IBS Indicator?
The IBS is a technical analysis tool that measures the relative position of a day’s closing price within its high-low range. It oscillates between 0 and 1.
A value near
0
indicates the close is near the day’s
low
.
A value near
1
indicates the close is near the day’s
high
.
The formula is straightforward: IBS = (Close – Low) / (High – Low)
For example, if the S&P 500 ETF (SPY) has a high of 338.25, a low of 326.13, and closes at 326.66, the IBS would be 0.043, a very low value suggesting the market is oversold for the short term.
The Strategy: Buy Weakness, Sell Strength
The strategy we show is to buy on weakness and sell on strength. The IBS is the cornerstone of mean-reverting strategies. The core assumption is that if a stock closes near its low (low IBS), it is “weak” and likely to bounce back, making it a bullish signal.
Conversely, closing near the high (high IBS) is considered bearish for the short term.

Why It Works Best in Bear Markets
Perhaps counterintuitively, the IBS indicator performs best during volatile or bear market conditions. This is because the largest “up days” often occur during bear markets, frequently driven by short covering.
In these high-uncertainty environments, mean-reverting behavior is often stronger. In fact, 2020, a year of extreme volatility, was one of the best years on record for this indicator.
Where It Fails: The Commodity Trap
While powerful, the IBS is not a “universal” indicator. Its effectiveness is largely limited to single stocks and broad-based stock indices.
It performs poorly in:
Commodity-specific sectors like gold and metals.
Markets heavily influenced by commodities, such as the Norwegian or Australian stock exchanges.
Every market has its own “ecology,” and the mean-reverting rules of the S&P 500 do not necessarily apply to gold.
The Practical Challenge: Execution
The primary weakness of the IBS indicator is execution timing. To capture the signal accurately, you must trade as close to the market close as possible.
The importance of the closing price is paramount: for the Nasdaq 100, delaying execution until the following day’s open causes the average gain per trade to drop from 0.9% to 0.75%.
Conclusion
The Internal Bar Strength indicator remains a potent tool for traders willing to trade near the close and focus on mean-reverting equity markets. While it can be enhanced by adding filters like the VIX or moving averages, its raw simplicity is exactly what has allowed it to endure for decades.
Strangely, the IBS indicator is a powerful indicator you have never heard of.
