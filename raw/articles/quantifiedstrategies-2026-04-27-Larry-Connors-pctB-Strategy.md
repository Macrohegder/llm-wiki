Today, we look at

, a simple mean-reversion strategy.
Mean reversion strategies have long been a cornerstone of short-term trading, and few have popularized them as effectively as Larry Connors. Among his many approaches, the
%B indicator strategy
stands out for its simplicity and practical edge.
At its core, the %B indicator is derived from Bollinger Bands. Instead of just showing price relative to the bands, %B converts that information into a normalized value, making it easier to spot extremes.
A %B value
above 1
means price is above the upper Bollinger Band
A %B value
below 0
means price is below the lower band
Connors’ insight was straightforward: extreme readings often precede short-term reversals.
The Basic Idea Behind %B Strategy
The %B strategy is built on a simple premise: markets tend to snap back after short-term overextension.
A typical setup looks like this (according to Connors):
Identify a market in a long-term uptrend (e.g., above the 200-day moving average)
Wait for %B to drop below 0, signaling an oversold condition
Enter long, anticipating a short-term rebound
Exit after a quick recovery (often within a few days)
This approach doesn’t try to predict big trends. Instead, it focuses on capturing small, repeatable gains from temporary dislocations.
Because this is mean reversion for the stock market, you have many winners and occasionally a nasty loser.
Why Mean Reversion Works
Markets are driven by human behavior, and fear and greed create short-term inefficiencies. When price stretches too far from its average, it often reverts.
The %B indicator helps quantify that stretch in a clean, objective way. Combined with a trend filter, it avoids fighting the broader market direction.
It’s worth mentioning that mean reversion works mainly for stocks, and not so well for other assets.
