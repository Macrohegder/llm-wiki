A trend following strategy for the S&P 500 is one of those ideas that sounds almost too simple to work: buy when the market is going up, sell (or step aside) when it’s going down.
Yet, despite its simplicity, it has quietly survived decades of market cycles, crashes, and booms.
At its core, it’s not about predicting the future. Instead, it’s about reacting to what the market is doing and staying aligned with that direction for as long as it persists.
The famous Turtle Experiment by Richard Dennis in the 1980s made trend following famous.
Trend Following Strategy for the S&P 500
What makes trend following especially interesting in the context of the S&P 500 is the market’s long-term upward bias combined with its occasional sharp drawdowns.
A trend follower doesn’t try to call tops or bottoms. Instead, the goal is to capture the “meat” of major moves while avoiding the worst of the declines. This often means accepting that you’ll never buy at the exact low or sell at the exact high, and being perfectly okay with that trade-off.
Trend-Following Strategy Drawbacks
Also, you must expect a low win rate and many whipsaws. This is the drawback, and makes it hard to follow the rules. For example, a low win rate means the probability of many consecutive losers rises significantly. Are you able to pull the trigger after 5 losses in a row?
What Makes Trend-Following Appealing?
Personally, I’ve always found trend following appealing because it removes the need for constant opinions. There’s no need to guess what the Fed will do next or whether valuations are stretched. The strategy forces a kind of discipline: follow the rules, trust the process, and let the market dictate your positioning.
It’s not flashy, but over time, that consistency is exactly what makes it powerful.
Furthermore, trend-following is a fantastic diversification from mean reversion strategies. These two types of strategies behave differently - a lot so.
If you are a serious trader, you should try to implement different strategies, market directions, time frames, and asset classes to trade.
