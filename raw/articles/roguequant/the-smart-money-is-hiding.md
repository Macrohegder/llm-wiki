Every Friday, the CFTC lifts the curtain on what the biggest players in futures markets did the previous Tuesday.

The CFTC breaks the market down into three groups (in the Legacy Report):

•Producers/Merchants (PM)are the commercials. The farmers, miners, refiners, and physical traders who use futures to hedge their real-world exposure. They’re considered the “smart money” in commodities because they know their market better than anyone (in theory of course).•Managed Money (MM)are the hedge funds and CTAs. Pure speculators chasing price, typically trend-followers.•Non-Reportables (NR)are small retail traders whose positions are too small to require individual reporting.

In financial futures, the cast changes slightly: Asset Managers (AM) replace Producers as the institutional anchor (think pension funds and sovereign wealth funds) while Leveraged Money (LM) takes the role of the speculative hedge fund money.

One way to  get context using the CoT report is from watching when these groups reach historical extremes because extremes tend to revert.


#### A Quick Note on Percentiles


Throughout this report, you’ll see references to percentiles:

> “Producers are at the 96th percentile” or “hedge funds are at the 20th    percentile.”


“Producers are at the 96th percentile” or “hedge funds are at the 20th    percentile.”

Here’s all you need to know to read them:

A percentile tells you where today’s positioning ranks compared to the last three years of history.

Think of it as a simple scale:

For example: the 73rd percentile means this group is more bullish (or bearish) than they were in 73% of all weeks over the past three years.

Relevant context but the market still has room to keep moving in the same direction. No alarm bells yet.

The signal starts getting genuinely interesting above 80–85th, and becomes critical above 92–95th, which is exactly where our dashboard triggers the “Crowded Long” and “Crowded Short” alerts.

The sweet spot for contrarian trades isn’t just about hitting a high percentile.

It’s about seeing one group at an extreme while the opposite group is at the other extreme, like Soybean Oil this week as you’re going to see in a moment, where hedge funds hit the 100th percentile while commercials sat at the 0th.

That’s when the setup becomes genuinely asymmetric and a signal worth paying attention to.

## COMMODITIES



#### CRUDE OIL: Bullish


The setup: The Producers/Commercials (think refineries, oil majors, physical traders who live and breathe crude) have a net position at the 96th percentile over the past three years.

In plain English: they haven’t been this bullish on their own product in three years.

These are the people who know the physical market better than anyone.

When they stop hedging downside aggressively, it usually means they don’t think prices are going lower.

At the same time, hedge funds (Managed Money) are only at the 54th percentile, right in the middle of their historical range.

That matters because it means there’s still plenty of room for speculative money to pile in.

The “crowded long” problem doesn’t apply here yet.

What happened over 13 weeks: MM added +115,000 contracts of net long exposure. That’s a sustained, methodical accumulation, not a panic spike.

Why it matters: When commercials are near their bullish extreme AND speculators still have room to buy, you get the classic CoT setup: informed money loaded up, trend-following money just starting to join.

The contrarian and trend signals are both pointing the same direction: bullish.

That alignment is rare and worth noting.

Watch: The open interest on WTI is massive at 3.1 million contracts.

Watch whether OI is expanding (new money entering) or contracting (people leaving) as price moves.


#### COPPER: Bearish


The setup: Here’s an interesting one.

Hedge funds (MM) are still net long copper at the 72nd percentile, moderately elevated.

But Producers are at just the 15th percentile, they’re hedging aggressively, which means they think prices might fall.

In commodity markets, when commercials hedge hard, it’s often because they see oversupply or weak demand ahead.

The trend is the tell: Over the past 13 weeks, hedge funds have quietly cut their long exposure by 28,000 contracts.

That’s not a crash-out but a slow, deliberate exit.

And the Trend score is a perfect -100, meaning the momentum in positioning is unambiguously to the downside.

What this means: Copper is a global growth barometer.

When the people mining and smelting it are aggressively hedging (essentially locking in current prices rather than exposing themselves to future prices) that’s a sign of caution about demand.

Between the producer hedging and the speculative exit, the CoT is not supportive of a copper rally right now.

Counterpoint: MM is still at the 72nd percentile. A full washout (sub-20th) hasn’t happened yet, which means the unwind could still have room to run.


#### SOYBEAN OIL — Warning: Crowded Long


The setup: This is the most extreme positioning in the entire CoT universe this week.

Hedge funds are at the 100th percentile. The longest they’ve been in three years, the absolute maximum.

And Producers (the crushers and processors who turn soybeans into oil) are at the 0th percentile, the most aggressively hedged (short) they’ve been in three years.

Every single data point in the lookback window sits above the current commercial position.

Over the past 13 weeks, MM added a jaw-dropping +217,000 contracts of net long exposure.

That’s not a trend, that’s a stampede.

Why “Crowded Long” is a red flag: This is the CoT equivalent of a fire exit that’s been locked. When positioning reaches this kind of extreme, the market becomes fragile.

It doesn’t need bad news to fall it just needs the buying to stop.

The moment hedge funds start taking profits or get stopped out, there’s no one left to absorb the selling.

And on the other side, commercials (who’ve been selling every rally) are ready to cover their hedges as prices fall, which accelerates the drop.

This is not a timing signal. Crowded longs can stay crowded for weeks (or months).

But the risk/reward is skewed: the upside is limited (who’s left to buy?) and the downside could be swift.

Keep this one on your radar for the washout trade.


#### PLATINUM: Bullish


The setup: Producers are at the 95th percentile. Nearly the least hedged they’ve been in three years.

For platinum miners and refiners to reduce their hedges this aggressively, they’re effectively betting on higher prices. That’s notable.

Hedge funds agree: MM has been steadily adding to longs over 13 weeks (+6,167 contracts), pushing to the 69th percentile. Both groups aligned in the same direction.

The nuance: Last 4 weeks, MM actually slowed down (+6,265 contracts but with a slightly negative Rate of Chage - RoC score). That suggests a pause, not a reversal.

Platinum’s open interest is small (64,000 contracts), which means moves can be sharp (and btw, next strategy will be about Platinum. Keep an eye on it next Monday).

The combination of commercial conviction and a consolidating speculative position could be the setup for the next leg up.


#### COFFEE: Bullish


The setup: Coffee is a contrarian’s dream right now. Hedge funds have retreated. MM is at just the 22nd percentile, near the low end of their 3-year range.

Meanwhile, Producers (the coffee exporters and traders) are at the 78th percentile, comfortably hedged but not panicking.

The signal: When speculators leave a market (especially after a big run like coffee had in 2024-25) and commercials hold steady, it often sets up the next wave.

MM is actually turning back up (+7,838 contracts in 4 weeks), suggesting the exit may be done and re-accumulation is beginning.

Context: Coffee had one of the biggest commodity rallies of the last decade.

The CoT pullback was healthy. Positioning has reset enough to make the contrarian case interesting again.


#### SILVER: Mild Bullish


The setup: Silver is flashing one of the cleanest contrarian signals in the complex. Producers are at the 97th percentile. Nearly as un-hedged as they’ve been in three years.

That’s a statement of confidence from the people who dig it out of the ground. Meanwhile, hedge funds are at just the 20th percentile which means they’ve almost entirely left the party.

The tension: The Trend score is negative (-33), meaning the speculative exit is still in motion.

This is the classic “falling knife vs. contrarian setup” dilemma.

The CoT data alone says: the informed money is bullish, the speculative money is leaving.

For trend followers: wait.

For contrarians: watch for the turn.

Gold context: worth noting that Gold’s commercial positioning is less extreme than Silver’s, making Silver’s setup the more interesting of the two precious metals from a CoT perspective right now.


## FINANCIAL FUTURES



#### US TREASURIES: The Bond Market is Screaming Risk-Off.


Something significant is happening in the bond market, and the CoT is loud about it.

10-Year T-Note:Asset Managers (the big pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds) are at the 95th percentile in net long bonds.

They haven’t been this long duration in three years.

Meanwhile, Leveraged Money (hedge funds) is at only the 29th percentile, meaning the fast money is actually on the other side or neutral.

2-Year T-Note:Asset Managers are at the 99th percentile. Nearly every data point in the last three years shows less institutional buying of 2-year paper than we’re seeing right now.

What this means: when the biggest, longest-horizon institutional investors load up on government bonds across the entire curve (both short and long end) simultaneously, that’s not a tactical trade.

That’s a structural repositioning. These managers are pricing in rate cuts, economic slowdown, or both.

They don’t move this aggressively without a reason.

The 30-Year Bond twist: Here’s where it gets interesting…

Leveraged Money on the 30-year T-Bond is at the 99th percentile long. A “Crowded Long” flag.

Hedge funds are crowded into long-duration bonds. That’s a positioning risk: if rates spike or inflation surprises to the upside, the unwind could be violent.


#### US EQUITIES: Institutions Are Quietly Reducing Risk


Russell 2000:Asset Managers are at just the 9th percentile of net exposure to small-cap stocks. They’ve almost entirely stepped away.

Small caps are typically the first to go in a risk-off rotation, and the institutional CoT is confirming exactly that.

Nasdaq 100:Asset Managers at the 19th percentile. Same story, slightly less extreme. The world’s biggest money managers are underweight US tech.

The picture: Institutions are simultaneously buying bonds at historic highs AND reducing equity exposure to historic lows.

That’s not an ambiguous signal.

Whether this is a leading indicator or a reflection of recent volatility, the positioning itself tells you where the big money stands.


#### NZ DOLLAR: Crowded Short


One FX note worth watching: the New Zealand Dollar has a “Crowded Short” flag, with Leveraged Money at just the 3rd percentile.

Hedge funds are about as short NZD as they’ve ever been in three years.

Crowded shorts, like crowded longs, are fragile. Any positive surprise from New Zealand’s economy or a broad USD weakening could spark a sharp short-covering rally.

Not a directional call, just a fragile positioning situation.


## CROSS-MARKET THEME: The Defensive Rotation


Pull back and look at all of this together.

Institutions are buying bonds (2Y at 99th, 10Y at 95th percentile).

Institutions are leaving equities (Russell at 9th, Nasdaq at 19th).

The commodity “smart money” is bullish on energy (WTI PM at 96th) and precious metals (Silver PM at 97th, Platinum PM at 95th).

Grain speculators are dangerously crowded in Soybean Oil.

This is a classic late-cycle defensive rotation playing out in real time across futures markets.

Whether it ends in a soft landing or something harder, the CoT data suggests the biggest players are not positioned for continued risk-on momentum.
