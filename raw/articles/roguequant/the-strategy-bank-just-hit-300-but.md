## Seasonality Without Statistics Is Just a Story


Seasonality is one of the oldest edges in futures trading.

Harvest cycles.Fiscal calendars.Institutional flows.Energy demand patterns.

These things repeat. Not perfectly. But enough to matter.

The problem isn’t the edge.

The problem is the work required to find it.

To do this properly you need to pull years of price data for a contract. Calculate the average return for each window. Check the win rate. Measure the Sharpe. Look at the worst years, not just the average. Then do it again for a different lookback period. Then for a different contract.

Most traders do one of two things instead.

They use a free seasonal chart with a single line and no statistics. Or they skip it entirely.

Neither is useful.

A single line tells you what happened on average. It doesn’t tell you how often it worked. How bad the losing years were. Whether the edge is consistent or just a few outliers pulling the average up.

That’s not analysis. That’s a vibe.

So I built something to replace the spreadsheet…


## The Tool I Built to Replace the Spreadsheet


It’s called…


#### TRQ Seasonal Lab.


It covers 30 futures contracts across indices, energy, metals, grains, bonds, currencies, crypto, softs, and meats.

All built on real price history analyzed statistically.

What actually happened, year by year, in each time window.

The app has five tools.

TheSeasonal Chartand Calendar Patterns lets you pick a contract and compare the average seasonal curve over the last 5, 10, 15, or 20 years against the current year. It’s the first thing I want to know before entering any trade: is the market moving with or against the historical pattern?

TheHeatmapgives you a fast way to scan all 30 contracts by month, so you can quickly see where historically strong tendencies tend to show up.

TheOpportunitiessection surfaces pre-defined seasonal windows with full statistics — average return, win rate, Sharpe, and maximum adverse excursion — so you can see which entry/exit periods have actually held up over time.

And theScreeneris the tool I use the most.

With entry fixed as today, it scans all 30 contracts and looks for the best seasonal exit points over the next 5 to 90 trading days.

You can filter by market, direction, lookback period, and minimum win rate.

Then you can expand any result and see the real year-by-year history behind it…

How much it made or lost each year, the cumulative return, the bar chart, the actual behavior over time.

It doesn’t generate buy and sell signals. It’s not a black box.

Seasonality is an edge…

A probability in your favor, not a guarantee.

You still need to think, manage risk, and decide whether to deploy it through an automated system or as an edge for your discretionary trading.

What the Seasonal Lab does is remove the manual work so you get to the meaningful question faster.
