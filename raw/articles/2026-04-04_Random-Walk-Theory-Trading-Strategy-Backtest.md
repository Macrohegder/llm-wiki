Today, we are doing a random walk theory trading strategy backtest.
Random walk theory and the efficient market hypothesis suggest price moves are largely unpredictable and that it’s extremely hard to ‘beat the market’ with timing or patterns.
A simple backtest shows that even completely random entry signals can deliver competitive returns over time, simply by riding the broad market’s long‑term upward drift.
This highlights how markets quickly price in information, making consistent outperformance via timing very unlikely and reinforcing the value of diversified, systematic investing.
Random Walk Theory Trading Strategy Backtest
We made the following trading rules:
We make 2-3 trades (buys) per month .
Every buy is generated randomly.
We exit after 5 trading days.

Our random walk trading strategy shows solid results on S&P 500 stocks from 2003 to today.
The equity curve looks strong, though much of the performance comes from the overall upward trend of the market.
Key stats are promising: the average gain per trade is 0.3%, the profit factor is 1.4, and the maximum drawdown is 48%. Annualized returns are 6.2%, even though the strategy is invested only 45% of the time. Adjusted for active exposure, the risk‑adjusted return is nearly 14%, outperforming buy-and-hold at 11%.
This is why it’s important to do a Monte Carlo analysis for every backtest you make.
