What are Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE) and Maximum Favorable Excursion (MFE)?
Every trader has experienced the anxiety of watching a trade move immediately into the red before eventually turning profitable.
Conversely, we’ve all watched a winning trade peak, only to see those profits evaporate before we finally hit “sell.”
To improve a trading strategy, you must move beyond just looking at the final win or loss; you need to understand the “excursions” that happen while the trade is live.
What Are Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE) and Maximum Favorable Excursion (MFE)?
These concepts were introduced by John Sweeney, the former Technical Editor of
Technical Analysis of Stocks and Commodities
magazine. His goal was to help traders use historical data to differentiate between normal price fluctuations and “abnormal” drawdowns that signal a trade is unlikely to recover.
Defining the Metrics
Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE)
MAE quantifies the maximum unrealized loss a trade experiences from the moment of entry to its lowest point (for long positions) or highest point (for short positions) before the trade is either closed or turns back in your favor.
Maximum Adverse Excursion (MAE)
For a Long Position:
MAE is the difference between your entry price and the lowest price reached during the trade.
For a Short Position:
MAE is the difference between your entry price and the highest price reached during the trade.
Maximum Favorable Excursion (MFE)
MFE measures the peak unrealized profit attained during a trade before it is closed. Essentially, it represents the maximum amount of money a trader
could
have earned if they had exited at the absolute “optimal moment”.

Why These Metrics Matter for Your Strategy
Experienced traders know that determining
how much to risk
is just as vital as knowing
when to trade
. MAE and MFE serve as essential risk management tools in several ways:
Refining Stop Losses:
By analyzing the MAE of a large sample of historical trades, you can identify the level of drawdown at which trades typically fail to recover. If your current stop-loss is consistently much wider than your strategy’s typical MAE, you might be able to shorten your stops, thereby improving your overall returns.
Optimizing Profit Targets:
MFE helps you see if you are “leaving money on the table”. If your average profit is significantly lower than the average MFE of your trades, it suggests that adjusting your Take Profit levels higher could capture more of the move.
Evaluating Risk-Reward:
Comparing these two metrics allows for a deeper evaluation of a trade’s efficiency. If your MAE is consistently much larger than your MFE, you are risking significantly more than you are potentially gaining, indicating a need to rethink your exit strategy.
Practical Application and Data Analysis
To use these metrics effectively, you cannot rely on just a few trades. A significant sample size is required to ensure the results are representative of market conditions and not just outliers. A sample size that is too small can underestimate risk and fail to account for extreme market behavior.
Practical application of MAE and MFE
Traders often represent this data using a scatter plot, which allows for a visual comparison of MAE and MFE against established Stop Loss and Take Profit levels. These metrics can be expressed in various formats depending on the trader’s preference, including:
Pips
Dollar amount per trade
Multiples of Risk (R)
Percentage (%)
The Psychological Edge
Understanding MAE is also a tool for trading psychology. Many traders abandon valid strategies because they cannot tolerate the “heat” or drawdowns of live trades. Knowing a system's historical MAE helps you to better tolerate inevitable losses and avoid emotional biases, such as selling at the bottom.
