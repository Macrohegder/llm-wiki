Today, we show you a momentum strategy for the S&P 500. But first, a primer on momentum.
What is a momentum strategy?
A momentum strategy is often described as the opposite of the “buy low, sell high” mantra; instead, it focuses on buying strength.
This approach is rooted in the momentum hypothesis, which suggests a strong correlation between a stock’s historical performance (over 3-12 months) and its future returns over the same period. For shorter or longer periods, mean reversion has worked better than momentum.
Why Momentum Works: The 80/20 Rule
Momentum works because you have few big winners.
The logic behind momentum investing is supported by the distribution of market returns. Research by Eric Crittenden suggests that over the long term, a small minority of stocks drive the vast majority of market gains. Specifically:
Approximately 20% of all stocks accounted for all market gains between 1989 and 2015.
The remaining 80% of stocks essentially provided zero returns or ended up worthless.
Only 7.7% of active stocks outperformed the S&P 500 by at least 500% during their lifetimes.
Since picking winners is notoriously difficult, a momentum strategy acts as a defensive filter: it keeps you invested in the few “multibaggers” while helping you avoid weak, underperforming stocks.
However, today we show you a momentum strategy for the S&P 500 - the index that shows the performance of the 500 biggest US companies.
The Trading Rules
The beauty of this strategy lies in its simplicity. It utilizes monthly bars and a simple moving average crossover system.
Buy Signal (Long):
Go long when the close of the current month crosses above its 12-month simple moving average (SMA).
Sell Signal (Exit):
Sell and stay in cash when the close of the current month crosses below its 12-month SMA.
It’s as simple as it gets.
You can implement these signals at the close of the month or at the opening of the following month. While different moving averages exist, backtesting indicates that the simple moving average outperforms the exponential moving average for this specific system.
Backtest Results (1960 – Present)
A backtest of this strategy using the S&P 500 cash index (excluding dividends) provides the following insights into its performance compared to a standard “buy and hold” approach:
Momentum strategy for the S&P 500
Growth of Capital:
A hypothetical 100,000 investment in 1960 would have grown to 8.2 million today.
Annual Return:
The strategy yielded a 6.9% annual return, slightly lower than the 7.5% for buy and hold.
Risk-Adjusted Return:
Because the strategy is only invested in the market 70% of the time, its risk-adjusted return is actually 9.8%, which is substantially higher than buy and hold.
Drawdown Protection:
The most significant advantage is capital preservation. The strategy’s maximum drawdown was only 26%, compared to a staggering
55%
for the S&P 500 index.
Trade Statistics:
Since 1960, the strategy has only made 39 trades. While there are losing trades, they are offset by the “big winners”: the average winner is nearly 25%, while the average loss is less than 5%.
Conclusion
While the absolute returns of this momentum strategy are slightly lower than holding the index, it offers a superior risk-adjusted profile.
The momentum strategy for the S&P 500 makes sure you are in cash when a major bear market comes around.
