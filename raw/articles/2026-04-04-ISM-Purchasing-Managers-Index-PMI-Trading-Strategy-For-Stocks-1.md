What Is The ISM Purchasing Managers Index (PMI)?
Introduced in 1948 by the Institute for Supply Management (ISM), the index is derived from a monthly survey of purchasing and supply executives across the country.
These professionals provide data on five key indicators: new orders, production, employment, supplier deliveries, and inventories, each of which is assigned an equal 20% weighting in the final composite index.
The results are converted into a diffusion index where a reading above 50 indicates sector expansion, while a reading below 50 indicates contraction.
What Is Regarded As A Good Reading For Stocks?
For stock market investors, a reading above 50 is generally regarded as a “good” or bullish signal. A rising PMI suggests that the manufacturing economy is growing, which typically leads to increased investor confidence, higher corporate profits, and increased capital investment.

Conversely, a falling PMI or a reading below 50 signals economic deterioration and a potential drop in stock prices as demand slows. While many traders view the PMI as a leading indicator of GDP growth, it is also considered a backward-looking indicator because it reflects past activity and may already be “yesterday’s news” by the time it is published.
