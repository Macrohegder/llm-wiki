Today, I show you the
RSI 30 50 strategy for beginners
, the first strategy I backtested back in 1999.
When I first started using RSI, I made the classic mistake: I treated 30 as oversold and 70 as overbought, and traded reversals blindly. It didn’t take long before I realized that in strong trends, RSI can stay “overbought” or “oversold” for a long time.
This was my first discovery back in 1999, when I dipped my toes into backtesting (using MetaStock).
That’s when I discovered a much more practical way to use RSI: the
30–50 zone
.
This simple shift improved the strategy.
This was my first mechanized trading strategy. It surely didn’t set the world on fire, but I began to understand the power of automation and backtesting. I hope it can help you too.
What the RSI 30–50 Zone Really Means
Instead of using RSI as a reversal tool, I used it as a pullback tool.
The key insight for me was this:
👉 The
30–50 zone is where pullbacks happen in an uptrend
How I Trade the RSI 30–50 Strategy
My approach is simple and rule-based (as always). I don’t try to overcomplicate it.
(And, by the way, the asset you choose to trade is more important than the strategy.)
1. Identify the Trend First
Only use this strategy in bullish markets.
Price above a rising moving average (like the 200-day) → uptrend
Price below a falling moving average → downtrend
Simple, but efficient.
2. Wait for RSI to Pull Back Into 30–50
In a bullish market:
Wait for RSI to drop below 30
This tells the market is pulling back, not reversing.
4. Enter the Trade
Once you get confirmation, enter long.
5. When To Sell
When the RSI reaches 50, sell.
I used a 5-day value (lookback period) of the RSI.
Why This Strategy Works
Instead of fighting the trend, you’ll:
Buying dips in an uptrend
The RSI 30–50 helps you time those entries better.
