The Profit Trap: Why Most Traders Are Measuring All Wrong
The trading world is obsessed with the top line.
"My strategy made 50% last year!"
"I doubled my account!"
These are common boasts, but they tell an incomplete, often misleading, story. They ignore the agonizing drawdowns, the capital trapped for months, and the psychological stress endured during deep losses.
This singular focus on profit leads to:
Unsustainable Strategies:
Systems that look good on paper but crumble under real market conditions.
Emotional Rollercoasters:
Large drawdowns that lead to panic selling or abandoning perfectly good strategies.
Misleading Comparisons:
Evaluating two strategies solely on CAGR, ignoring that one might have taken a 70% drawdown to achieve those returns, while the other had a small 10% dip.
Until you account for the risk taken to generate those returns, you're flying blind.
The MAR Ratio: Your Navigator in the Volatile Seas of Trading
The MAR Ratio, named after the
Managed Accounts Reports
newsletter in 1978 where it was introduced by Leon Rose, is a sophisticated measure of risk-adjusted returns. It directly addresses the shortcomings of solely focusing on compound annual growth rate (CAGR) by incorporating the maximum drawdown (MDD) into the equation.
Here's why the MAR Ratio is revolutionary:
Compares Apples to Apples:
Allows you to compare the performance of vastly different trading strategies or portfolios on an even playing field, considering both return and risk.
Identifies Robustness:
Highlights strategies that not only generate returns but do so with controlled risk, indicating greater resilience.
Promotes Realistic Expectations:
Shifts focus from chasing explosive, often unsustainable, gains to achieving consistent, risk-mitigated growth.
Aligns with Long-Term Survival:
Strategies with better MAR Ratios are more likely to survive market downturns and retain capital, ensuring you stay in the game.
How the MAR Ratio is Calculated
The elegance of the MAR Ratio lies in its simplicity once you understand its components.
Let's break down each part:
Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR):
This is the mean annual growth rate of your strategy or portfolio over a specified period longer than one year. It shows the smoothed annual return.
Convert this rate into a percentage for the MAR Ratio calculation (0.15 for 15%).
Maximum Drawdown (MDD):
The MDD represents the largest peak-to-trough decline in the value of your strategy or portfolio before a new peak is achieved. It’s the worst-case capital loss you would have experienced at any point.
Example:
If your account goes from $100,000 down to $60,000 (a $40,000 loss) before recovering and hitting new highs, your MDD is $40,000 / $100,000 = 0.40 or 40%.
Convert this ratio into a positive percentage for the MAR Ratio calculation (0.40 for 40%).
Finally, let's calculate the MAR ratio:
Example Scenario:
Strategy A:
CAGR of 20% (0.20), Max Drawdown of 25% (0.25) MAR Ratio = 0.20 / 0.25 =
0.80
Strategy B:
CAGR of 30% (0.30), Max Drawdown of 50% (0.50) MAR Ratio = 0.30 / 0.50 =
0.60
Strategy C:
CAGR of 15% (0.15), Max Drawdown of 10% (0.10) MAR Ratio = 0.15 / 0.10 =
1.50
Notice how Strategy C, despite having a lower absolute return than Strategy B, has a significantly higher MAR Ratio because it kept its drawdowns tightly controlled. Strategy A, with its respectable 0.80, shows a better risk-adjusted return than Strategy B, even with lower gross gains. This illustrates the power of the MAR Ratio – it forces you to consider the often-overlooked aspect of risk.
What is a "Good" MAR Ratio? Setting Your Benchmarks
Defining a "good" MAR Ratio isn't a one-size-fits-all answer. It heavily depends on whether you're evaluating a single trading strategy or a diversified portfolio of strategies.
For Individual Strategies: Expect Realism
You shouldn't design a strategy
solely
to maximize its MAR Ratio, as this often leads to curve-fitting. The MAR Ratio is best used as a diagnostic and comparative tool
after
a strategy is developed.
Based on extensive backtesting and my experience with over 800 strategies:
Below 0.2:
Often indicates a strategy with poor risk-adjusted returns, high drawdowns for the gains achieved, or insufficient data.
0.2 - 0.4:
This is where many robust, practical strategies tend to fall. It represents a reasonable balance of return and risk control in real-world trading.
0.5 and Above:
For me, this is the minimum threshold for a robust strategy I'd seriously consider adding to a portfolio. Achieving a 0.5 MAR Ratio with a solid track record is incredibly difficult and signifies excellent risk management.
1.0+:
Incredibly rare for a single, standalone robust strategy. If you consistently find strategies above 1.0, re-examine your data or assumptions; they might be over-optimized.
It's crucial to remember that a strategy with a lower MAR Ratio (like 0.3) might still be valuable if it offers diversification benefits to your overall portfolio or targets a specific market inefficiency not covered by other robust strategies.

For a Portfolio of Strategies: Aim for Excellence
Here's where the MAR Ratio truly shines and where your targets should be significantly higher. A well-constructed portfolio consists of strategies with different timeframes, methodologies, and underlying assets. This diversification allows you to:
Reduce Overall Drawdown:
When one strategy is down, another might be up or flat, smoothing out the equity curve.
Improve Consistency:
Less volatile returns due to uncorrelated strategies working together.
Because of your ability to combine strategies and actively manage risk across multiple fronts, your portfolio should be able to achieve a much higher MAR Ratio than any single strategy within it.
My benchmark for a well-diversified, actively managed portfolio:
1.0+ MAR Ratio (Minimum Target):
This should be the goal for any serious trading portfolio. Consistently achieving a MAR Ratio above 1.0 means you are generating more in annual returns than your worst historical drawdown, indicating superior risk management and capital preservation.
1.5+ MAR Ratio:
Excellent performance, showing exceptional strategy selection and portfolio construction.
2.0+ MAR Ratio:
World-class. This often signifies a highly sophisticated approach to risk management, strategy diversification, and potentially, unique market access or alpha generation.
Why the MAR Ratio Just
Works
The MAR Ratio's effectiveness stems from a simple, yet profound, truth:
“surviving drawdowns is just as, if not more, important than generating returns.”
It forces you to confront the psychological pain of losing capital. A high MAR Ratio means your journey will be smoother, less stressful, and your capital will be more readily available for future opportunities. This promotes:
Sustainable Growth:
Consistent gains with manageable dips are far more conducive to long-term wealth building than volatile swings.
Mental Fortitude:
Knowing your risk is controlled empowers you to stick to your plan during inevitable losing periods.
Compounding Power:
Smaller drawdowns mean your capital is compounding from a higher base, amplifying returns over time.
Context is Key: Using MAR for Comparison
While the MAR Ratio is powerful, it's primarily a comparative tool. Use it to:
Compare similar strategies:
Evaluate two trend-following systems on the same market, for instance, to see which manages risk better.
Benchmark against indices:
Compare your portfolio's MAR Ratio to that of a benchmark index or a traditional investment portfolio.
It's not ideal for comparing strategies in vastly different asset classes or with wildly different risk profiles (e.g., a high-frequency equity strategy vs. a long-term commodity strategy) in isolation, as their underlying market dynamics differ too much.

The MAR Ratio is One of Your Best Friends:
It's the most critical metric for evaluating risk-adjusted performance, offering a holistic view missing from simple return metrics.
Benchmarks Differ:
Aim for
0.5+

1.0+

Risk Control is Paramount:
The MAR Ratio emphasizes that managing drawdowns is as important as gross returns for sustainable growth.
Use it Comparatively:
Apply the MAR Ratio to compare similar strategies or to benchmark your portfolio's efficiency.
Evaluate Your Own:
Take the time to calculate the MAR Ratio for your existing strategies and portfolio. You might be surprised by what you find!
