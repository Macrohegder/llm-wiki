Today, we look at what happens to stocks when bond yields go down.
We show that almost all the gains in stocks occur when yields go down.
The Relationship Between Stocks And Bonds
The fundamental relationship between stocks and bonds is driven by interest rates, which directly influence investor risk appetite.
Bond prices and interest rates have an inverse relationship: when bond prices go up, yields (interest rates) go down.
When interest rates decline, stocks generally become more attractive for several reasons:
Valuation:
Lower rates mean investors are willing to pay more for a company’s future cash flows, often leading to higher P/E ratios.
Risk Appetite:
As yields on “safe” assets like bonds drop, investors move into riskier assets like stocks, gold, and Bitcoin to find better returns.
Relative Safety:
While bonds are inherently less risky than stocks because bondholders are paid before equity owners during a bankruptcy, rising bond prices signal an environment where owning stocks is more favorable.
The correlation between these assets is not constant and can vary, though it tends to strengthen during periods of high inflation.
