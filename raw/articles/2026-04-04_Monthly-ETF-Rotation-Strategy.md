Today, we present a monthly ETF rotation strategy.
One of the most compelling investing ideas is ETF rotation: owning only the strongest asset instead of holding everything at once.
Here’s what the data shows. But first, let’s look at the trading rules.
The idea behind an ETF rotation strategy
The concept is straightforward.
Instead of trying to predict the future, the strategy asks a simple question:
Which asset is strongest right now?
Capital is allocated to the strongest asset, and the process is repeated at regular intervals.
Each month, we rank the ETFs by percentage performance over the previous month.
The best-performing asset is bought and held for one month - then the ranking is updated, and the process starts again.
Why rotation works
Asset classes rarely outperform simultaneously.
Capital constantly rotates between risk-on and risk-off environments:
• equities during economic expansion
• defensive assets during uncertainty
• diversifiers during market stress
A rotation model systematically follows these capital flows rather than trying to forecast them. Historically, momentum has worked for short term periods of 1-12 months, but not longer.
Monthly ETF rotation strategy results
The strategy rotates between major ETFs based on relative performance. We first tested variations of this idea more than a decade ago using the following assets:
S&P 500 (SPY)
Long-term bonds (TLT)
Emerging markets (EEM)
Why did we include EEM?
Most developed markets are highly correlated, while emerging markets tend to provide more diversification.

The strategy worked reasonably well, but struggled more during the 2020s.
However, changing one parameter made a significant difference.
Instead of ranking assets based on the last 1 month, we used performance over the previous 3 months:

Interestingly, the 1-month lookback period produced the weakest results. Slightly longer momentum windows improved both returns and risk.
Final thoughts
ETF rotation is not magic. Whipsaws will occur, and periods of underperformance are unavoidable.
But the historical evidence points to an important lesson:
You don’t need to predict the market.
Momentum strategies using lookback periods between 1 and 12 months have worked for more than a century and are supported by extensive academic research.
The ETFs used here may not deliver the highest possible returns, but they illustrate a simple framework you can adapt and improve yourself.
