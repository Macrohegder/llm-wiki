The hidden cost of discretionary trading wasn't emotional. It was time.

For about a year, I was glued to Crude Oil charts. Eyes on the screen, plan ready, finger hovering. And honestly? It worked.
I was consistently profitable which is more than most traders can say and in the beginning of 2022 I had my best 6 month win-streak.
But by late 2022, something shifted. The strategy that had worked stopped working or at least it felt that way. A drawdown started and I had no idea what to do with that.
“Did this strategy just stop working? How do I even know that?”
That question haunted me. And it’s the question that eventually pushed me to stop trading discretionary entirely.
The Real Cost Nobody Talks About
Everyone talks about the emotional cost of discretionary trading. The stress, the second-guessing, the tilt. That’s all real.
But the cost that broke me wasn’t emotional. It was time.
Trading Crude manually required the same focus and hours as a second full-time job. Not occasionally, every single day. You can be disciplined and still be exhausted. I was executing well, but I had almost no energy left to actually think about trading. To improve. To step back and ask bigger questions.
Execution was eating everything. There was no time left for the parts I actually cared about: Building an edge. Exploring new ideas. Expand my trading strategy database.
The Drawdown That Changed Everything
When the drawdown hit, my first instinct was to ask: is this normal variance or is the strategy broken?
With discretionary trading, that question is almost impossible to answer cleanly. Your rules live in your head. Your historical sample size is whatever you can remember. The backtesting is a rough mental sketch.
I didn’t have the framework to answer that question with any confidence. So I paused.
And in that pause, I started looking at systematic trading seriously for the first time.
My First System Was a Disaster (And That Was Fine)
Around this time I came across algo traders online selling their systems. Equity curves going straight up. Monthly returns that looked too good to be true. Spoiler:
they were.
I didn’t know what overfitting was yet. I didn’t know that a backtest with 50 parameters and five years of data could produce almost any result you wanted. I just saw those results and thought…
I can build something like this.
So I did.
My first system had too many parameters, too much code and was completely overfit to historical data. The backtest said some insane result. I genuinely thought I’d found the answer.
“I remember thinking: I’ve just found my retirement. This strategy has performed perfectly for the last 5 years.”
Of course, the moment you trade something like that live, reality arrives quickly.
But here’s the thing:
That mistake was one of the most valuable things that happened to me.
Because I made it early and I made it hard enough that the lesson stuck. I spent the next months properly understanding what overfitting actually is, why it happens, and how to build systems that are actually robust. Most of that knowledge came directly from having my face pressed against the wrong approach first.
What Actually Changed When I Went Systematic
The most unexpected change wasn’t performance. It was mentality.
When execution becomes automatic, you get your thinking time back. Instead of spending every available hour watching a chart, I could spend it building, testing, questioning, learning.
The shift from trading discretionary to trading systematic wasn’t just a change in methodology, it was a change in what my time was actually for.
My years of discretionary trading weren’t wasted, either. Coming in with real trading experience meant I could start by looking at the chart and finding patterns that actually made sense, not just noise. I think that foundation made the transition faster and the systems better.
After about 1 year of intense learning I was consistently profitable again, but this time it was automated.
I also finally had a real answer to the question that started all of this: did the strategy stop working?
With a properly built and backtested system, that question has an actual framework. You can define what normal drawdown looks like. You can set thresholds. You know what variance to expect. You’re not guessing.
Should You Make the Switch?
Discretionary trading isn’t wrong. Plenty of people do it well and the instincts you build trading manually are genuinely useful, especially if you ever move toward building systems.
But if you find yourself asking any of these questions, it might be worth considering a more systematic approach:
How much of your day does execution actually take?
Could you clearly define your rules if someone asked you to?
When you hit a drawdown, do you have any real way to know if it’s normal?
For me, the answers made the decision obvious. The switch gave me my time back, forced me to think more thoughtfully about edge and after the painful lesson of my first overfitted system built a foundation that I actually trust.
