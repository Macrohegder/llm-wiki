Percentage of Profitable Day Traders vs Losers - Case Studies
The struggle for profitability persists even among those who treat trading as a professional business.
Proprietary traders typically perform better than retail traders, yet they still face high failure rates.
For example, a court case involving Tuco Trading revealed that out of 206 active traders, only 16% were profitable, while 84% lost money.
Another study of Broadway Trading showed that while profitability could reach as high as 81% during the dot-com bubble, it dropped significantly to 42% in the following years.
Both studies are old, but recent studies confirm the findings.
Global studies further illustrate these low success rates:
Brazil
: A study of 1,600 traders found that only 3% made money over the course of a year, and only 1.1% earned more than the minimum wage.
Taiwan
: Research conducted between 1992 and 2006 concluded that about 15% of traders made more than their commission costs, but less than 1% were able to predictably and reliably earn positive returns net of fees.
What about crypto?
Research analyzing millions of trades on Binance showed that high-frequency retail traders underperformed lower-frequency traders.
More trades ≠ more profits.
Transaction costs quietly eat the edge.

The authors of the study cite several reasons for the lackluster results:
The primary reasons for these failures include overconfidence and the market's inherent volatility.
Short-term trading is essentially a zero-sum game, or even a “minus-sum” game once commissions and slippage are factored in, meaning that for one person to win, another must lose. Unlike long-term investors, who benefit from the “tailwinds” of corporate growth and earnings, day traders do not have these advantages.
