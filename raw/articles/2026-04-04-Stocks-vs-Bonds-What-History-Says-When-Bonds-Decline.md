What does history say about stocks when bonds decline. Let’s look at it empirically.
Financial theory suggests that rising interest rates should hurt equities. Instead of relying on theory alone, we decided to test this idea with a historical backtest (explained further below).
Why do stocks often struggle when bonds decline?
Stocks and interest rates tend to have an inverse relationship, and the mechanics of bonds help explain why.
A bond pays a fixed coupon throughout its life until maturity. This coupon is typically paid annually as compensation to investors for lending money to the issuer. When the bond matures, the issuer repays the principal, or face value, to the investor.
For example, a bond with a 5% coupon pays 5% annually until maturity — say, over 20 years. A one-billion-dollar bond with a 5% coupon would therefore distribute 50 million dollars per year to bondholders.
However, interest rates change continuously during a bond’s lifetime. If market rates fall, a bond paying 5% becomes more attractive, and investors bid up its price. The price must adjust upward so that the bond’s yield aligns with the new, lower rates. For instance, if rates decline to 4.5%, the bond price might rise by roughly 10% (a simplified illustration - real pricing depends on several factors).
As rates change, newly issued bonds reflect current market yields. Existing bonds, therefore, become more or less attractive depending on whether their coupons are higher or lower than prevailing rates.
Falling bonds are not good for stocks — evidence from a backtest
We ran a historical test using:
• SPY (S&P 500 ETF) as a proxy for stocks
• TLT (20-year Treasury bond ETF) as a proxy for bonds
The idea is straightforward: when bonds fall (TLT trades below a moving average), interest rates are rising. We tested the following rules:
Buy SPY when TLT falls below its N-day moving average.
Sell SPY when TLT moves back above the moving average.
What happens if we stay invested in stocks during periods of falling bond prices?
The results were weak. Across multiple tests using moving averages from 5 to 100 days, stock performance during declining bond markets was consistently worse than the market’s average return.

In short, owning stocks while bonds decline has historically been an unfavorable environment for equities.
Flipping the logic - owning stocks when bonds are strong - produced significantly better results.
